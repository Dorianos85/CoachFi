## 1. Poland's Financial Context

Poland transitioned from a low-income planned economy to a high-income market economy over 25 years — unprecedented growth, record-low unemployment. But macro-level prosperity has NOT translated into personal financial confidence.

**Trust deficit (critical):**
- 2022: only **15% of Poles** trust financial institutions for savings/investments
- 2021: bottomed at **6%** — only 6% trusted financial sector employees

**Three structural challenges:**
- Demographic Aging: aging society, shrinking workforce → more pressure on pension system → individuals must take more responsibility
- Rural-Urban Disparities: significant income gaps, higher financial exclusion risk in rural areas
- Geopolitical Shocks: COVID-19 + Russian invasion of Ukraine → 4M+ refugees → energy price spikes, supply chain disruptions

## 2. OECD/INFE 2020 — Poland's Scores

Financial Competency = knowledge + skills + attitudes + behaviors for sound decisions.

| Metric | Poland | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Total Financial Competency | **13.0 / 21** | Average of 12 OECD countries |
| Financial Well-being | **9.1 / 20** | OECD-11 average: 9.9 / 20 |
| Bank Account Ownership | **96%** (age 15+) | High inclusion |

Score of 13 = operating without full "financial toolkit." Missing 8 points = lost wealth opportunities + lack of preparedness.

---

## 3. Knowledge Gaps — What Poles Know vs. Don't Know

**~75% of Poles understand:**
- Risk and Return: the link between potential profit and loss
- Basic Inflation: purchasing power diminishes as prices rise
- Risk Diversification: safety found in spreading investments

**CRITICAL GAP — Compound Interest:**
- Only **~1/3 of Poles** (33%) correctly understand how compound interest works in practice
- Why it matters: leads to "debt spirals" — interest accumulates faster than borrowers realize → insolvency
- Direct impact: primary reason PPE/PPK participation is only **47% among employees in large firms**

---

## 4. Financial Psychology — Attitudes and Well-being

**Financial Attitude score:**
- Only **26.9% of Poles** reach the minimum target score
- That's roughly **half the OECD average**
- Root cause: short-term thinking as psychological defense mechanism — when people feel no agency, they focus on immediate survival

**Financial Well-being = 3 components:**
1. **Control** — managing daily expenses, avoiding high-interest debt
2. **Safety Net (poduszka finansowa)** — financial cushion to absorb unexpected shocks without borrowing
3. **Future Goals** — establishing and staying on track for long-term milestones (especially retirement)

**Key finding:** Nearly **half of Poles** report that their finances control their lives (not the other way around).

## 5. Critical Competency Pillars

| Economic Reality | Required Competency |
|---|---|
| High Inflation | Understanding REAL rate of return to protect savings from erosion |
| Digital Transformation | Cybersecurity awareness — safely navigate online services, avoid fraud |
| Low Retirement Confidence | Understanding PPE/PPK voluntary pension schemes |

---

## 6. Target Groups Most At Risk

- **Low-income households** — rural areas, multi-child families, no emergency cushion
- **Precarious workers** — unstable/short-term contracts, unpredictable income
- **The Elderly** — fixed-income erosion, digital exclusion in cashless society
- **Youth and Parents** — PISA shows socio-economic disparities; gender gap: boys outperform girls in financial knowledge

---

## 7. KSEF — National Strategy for Financial Education

Goal: reduce information asymmetry, move from fragmented commercial initiatives to unified national framework.

**Financial Resilience** = the ability to deal with adverse financial shocks and recover from a difficult material situation.

Key actions:
- Unified non-commercial portal — "source of truth" for financial information
- Framework of Key Competencies — essential skills at every life stage
